Arbitration
Arbitration, like mediation, is a form of alternative dispute resolution. Like
mediation, arbitration requires the presence of a third party to help the settle the
conflict; this third party is called the arbiter. However, unlike mediators who
sometimes merely facilitate dialogue, arbiters are required to make a judgment on
the case, and their judgment is final and binding. Because of this, arbiters must have
expertise on law, adjudication and when applicable, company policy.
In order to have a constructive conflict resolution procedure through arbitration, it’s important that
many key issues regarding the process are settled before the arbitration proper. For instance, it must be
decided and agreed-upon how the arbiter is going to judge the matter: Is he or she going to weigh
interests? Would the arbiter be using a particular contract or policy for reference? Making this
information clear from the onset can help ensure fairness and prevent allegations of being placed at a
disadvantage. 
Like mediation, arbitration is a less costly alternative to going to court. It also tends to be speedier and
more private.  
There is a big risk in arbitration however. In an ideal world, parties in conflict must volunteer for the
process. However, in actual practice, there is the possibility of persons in power bullying the
disadvantaged to sit in an arbitration procedure and passively accept its results.
